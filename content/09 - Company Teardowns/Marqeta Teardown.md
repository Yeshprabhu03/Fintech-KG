# Marqeta Teardown: The Modern Issuer Processor

> ⚠️ Draft — needs verification against primary sources

Marqeta (NASDAQ: MQ) is the developer-centric engine behind some of the world’s most innovative card programs. Founded in 2010 by Jason Gardner, Marqeta spent years building a "modern issuer processor" from scratch. Before Marqeta, a company wanting to issue cards had to work with legacy incumbents like **TSYS**, **FIS**, or **Fiserv**—companies whose tech was built for plastic cards and batch processing. Marqeta changed the paradigm by offering a cloud-native, API-first platform that turned card issuing into a software problem.

## 1. What is an Issuer Processor?
In the four-party payment model, the **Issuer Processor** sits on the bank's side of the transaction. When you swipe a card at a merchant, the transaction travels through the merchant's bank (Acquirer) and the Network (Visa/Mastercard) to the Issuer Processor. It is the Processor's job to:
1. Verify the card data.
2. Check if there are sufficient funds.
3. Apply fraud filters.
4. Approve or decline the transaction in milliseconds.

While legacy processors were "black boxes" with rigid rules, Marqeta built a platform that allows developers to define their own authorization logic in real-time.

## 2. JIT (Just-in-Time) Funding: The Killer Feature
Marqeta’s most significant technical innovation is **JIT Funding**. In a traditional card model, you have to "pre-fund" an account before you can swipe the card. For a company like **DoorDash** or **Instacart**, this would be a nightmare—having to manage billions of dollars across thousands of delivery drivers' individual accounts.

JIT Funding flips this. The driver's card has a **$0.00 balance** until the moment of the swipe. When the card is swiped at the grocery store:
1. Marqeta sends a real-time "Gateway Request" to DoorDash’s own server.
2. DoorDash’s logic checks: "Is this driver at the correct store? Is the order value correct?"
3. If approved, DoorDash sends a "Yes" back to Marqeta.
4. Marqeta instantly "funds" the card and approves the transaction.

This happens in less than a second, eliminating the need for pre-funding and drastically reducing the risk of employee fraud or incorrect spending.

## 3. Dynamic Spend Controls
Beyond JIT Funding, Marqeta allows for granular, **dynamic spend controls**. A card can be restricted to:
- **Merchant Category Code (MCC)**: "Only works at restaurants."
- **Merchant ID (MID)**: "Only works at a specific Chipotle location."
- **Time/Duration**: "Only works between 5 PM and 7 PM today."

This level of control is what enabled the "gig economy" business models to scale. Without it, the risk of drivers using company cards for personal gas or groceries would have been too high to manage.

## 4. The API Shift: Marqeta vs. The Legacy Giants
Marqeta’s success was driven by the "Developer First" movement. 
- **Legacy Processors (TSYS/FIS)**: Required months of negotiation, physical hardware integrations, and the use of legacy messaging formats (ISO 8583) that were difficult for modern web developers to parse.
- **Marqeta**: Offered a REST API, webhooks, and a "sandbox" environment where a developer could issue a virtual card and test an authorization in minutes.

By commoditizing the complex "Bank-to-Network" connection, Marqeta allowed non-fintech companies (like **Uber**, **Klarna**, and **Affirm**) to incorporate financial services into their primary applications—a movement now known as **Embedded Finance**.

## 5. Revenue Model: Sharing the Swipe
Marqeta’s revenue is primarily linked to volume:
- **Interchange Sharing**: Marqeta takes a cut of the interchange revenue generated by its customers' cards.
- **Processing Fees**: Small, fixed fees per transaction or per active card.
- **Platform Fees**: Subscription fees for access to high-end features like JIT Funding.

## 6. The "Block" Concentration Risk
The primary concern for Marqeta investors is its customer concentration. **Block (formerly Square)**, specifically his **Cash App** and **Square Card** products, has historically accounted for **70-80%** of Marqeta’s total revenue. 
When Marqeta renewed its contract with Block in 2023, the terms were reportedly much less favorable for Marqeta, leading to a significant revenue "reset" and a drop in the stock price. This highlighted the vulnerability of infrastructure players when their primary customer gains "too much" leverage.

## 7. Competition & Post-IPO Challenges
Since Marqeta’s IPO in 2021, the landscape has become crowded:
- **Galileo (owned by SoFi)**: A major competitor in the neobanking space (powers Chime and Dave).
- **Lithic / Stripe Issuing**: Targeted at smaller startups and developers who want even simpler integrations.
- **Legacy Modernization**: TSYS and FIS are finally upgrading their stacks to compete with Marqeta's API-first approach.

Marqeta’s challenge is to diversify its revenue away from Block and expand into new geographies and products (like **Credit Card as a Service**) to justify its valuation as a high-growth tech platform.
